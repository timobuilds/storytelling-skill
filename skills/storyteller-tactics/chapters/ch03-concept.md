# Chapter 3: Concept Tactics

## Core Idea
Concept tactics answer *why you need a story*: frame the work as adventure, conflict, mystery, morality, or universal pattern so people care.

## Frameworks Introduced

- **The Dragon & the City** (Desert Island): Status quo = City (safe, limited); threat/opportunity = Dragon. Choose Escape / Defend / Attack.
  - When to use: Get people excited about a plan.
  - How: Define City (good/bad/who's in charge) → Dragon (threat, how it grew, gold/opportunity) → Escape / Defend / Attack options with costs → land with What's It About?

- **Three Great Conflicts**: Hero vs Nature, vs Society, vs Self. Map Dream ↔ Nightmare; put the greatest conflict at the story's heart.
  - When to use: Raise stakes; find the fight that drives the plot.

- **Order & Chaos** (credit: Peterson, *Maps of Meaning*): Known (orderly, safe, restricted) vs Unknown (chaotic, risky, potential). Hero balances both.
  - When to use: Clarify mission — impose order or disrupt rigidity.
  - Tip: Also tell from the user's POV as Hero.

- **Secrets & Puzzles**: Information gaps as attention hooks.
  - Secrets: you own missing info (keywords: secret, exclusive, untold…).
  - Puzzles: anomaly / irony / inconsistency (keywords: paradox, mystery…).
  - Rule: If you promise a secret/puzzle, deliver — otherwise it's clickbait.

- **Rules, Cheats & Rebels**: Attention from rule-breaking.
  - Rebel: selfless, higher rule → "this rule no longer holds."
  - Cheat: selfish, hides → "this rule still applies."
  - Pair innovator stories with Trust Me, I'm an Expert.

- **Good & Evil** (credit: Haidt, *The Righteous Mind*): Six moral foundations — care/harm, fairness/cheating, liberty/oppression, authority/subversion, loyalty/betrayal, purity/filth.
  - Simple: pick a side. Complex (Hegel): two rights collide / lesser of two evils.

- **Universal Stories** (credit: Brown, *Human Universals*): Free will; conflict & cooperation; inconsistencies; fear & bravery; right & wrong; rites of passage.

- **Curious Tales**: Curiosity (anomaly + benefit), Caution (anomaly + threat), Flow, Envy (healthy), Sacrifice — explain what drives you or your user.

- **Circle of Life** (Jungian archetypes): Child / Adult / Parent across Home → Wide World → Home. Story via Conflict, Change (rite of passage), or Character (fit vs fight the circle).

- **Shock of the Old** (Taleb, *Antifragile*): Old objects/habits that survived generations encode deep purpose — mine them for insight instead of only futurism.

## Key Concepts
- **City / Dragon**: Status quo vs threat-opportunity metaphor.
- **Information gap**: What audience doesn't know but wants to.
- **Moral foundations**: Six axes for Good & Evil stories.

## Mental Models
- Use Dragon & City when people need a shared epic frame.
- Use Secrets & Puzzles for intros, headlines, email subjects.
- Use Good & Evil when the work is really a moral choice.
- Use Circle of Life when the user is growing into a new role.

## Anti-patterns
- **Clickbait secrets** without payoff.
- **Pure villain/hero** when the real conflict is two rights.
- **Future-only framing** that ignores surviving old wisdom.

## Key Takeaways
1. Concept cards create meaning before craft.
2. Conflict (Nature/Society/Self or moral foundations) is the engine.
3. Promise gaps only if you close them.
4. Universal patterns make niche work feel familiar.
5. Desert Island Concept card: **The Dragon & the City**.
