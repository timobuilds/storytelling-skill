# Chapter 7: Structure Tactics

## Core Idea
Structure tactics answer *how to plan the story*: named arcs and beats that carry the audience, plus failure frames and audience-risk matching.

## Frameworks Introduced

- **Man in a Hole** (Desert Island; Vonnegut): Comfort Zone → Trigger → Crisis (treasure in the dark) → Recovery → Better Place. "Nobody ever lost money telling the story of a man in a hole." Great for Simple Sales Stories and lessons learned.

- **Rags to Riches**: Hidden Value → Trigger & Struggle → Deserved Recognition. Optimistic; about *value* not money. If it sounds too good, switch to No Easy Way. You may be Fairy Godmother (Hero & Guide).

- **No Easy Way**: Problem → Early Success → Setback → Crisis → Recovery → Better Place. Realistic rollercoaster; compare to POPP. Join the user's journey at the right beat (be Dumbledore, not Harry).

- **Pride & Fall** (Icarus pattern): Bad Place → Pride → Warning → Fall → Worse Place. Use to *pre-empt sceptics* who hear "too good to be true" when you tell Rags to Riches. Reassure with No Easy Way.

- **Downfall**: Flawed Power → Desperate Denial → Deserved Disgrace.
  - **Defend**: your flaws; learn; change.
  - **Exploit**: rival's flaws; show customers deserve better.

- **Epic Fail** (Mantere et al.): Six attribution stories after failure — Catharsis, Hubris, Betrayal, Mechanistic, Zeitgeist, Nemesis. Use to think differently, not to play the blame game. Compare Thoughtful Failures.

- **Innovation Curve** (Rogers, *Diffusion of Innovations*): Match story to risk thermostat —
  | Segment | Tell them |
  |---------|-----------|
  | Innovators (you) | Team culture (Drive Stories) |
  | Pioneers | Process, breakthroughs, prototypes |
  | Early Mainstream | Niche lead; tested |
  | Late Mainstream | Works in niche → ready for mass |
  | Traditional | Everyone's doing it; we made it easy |

- **Voyage & Return** (Booker, *Seven Basic Plots*): Home (safe/dull) → Voyage (strange world) → Return (changed hero, changed home). Pair with Order & Chaos.

- **Happy Ever Afters**: Ending = Hero changes by Growing Up, Finding Home/Love/Respect, or Doing the Right Thing. Often write the end first; use Five Ts / Circle of Life / Voyage & Return.

- **Five Ts** (pocket structure for on-the-spot stories):
  - **Timeline** (before/after or beginning/middle/end)
  - **Turning Points** (realisation, decision, change)
  - **Tensions** (start at max anxiety)
  - **Temptations** (easy way vs right thing)
  - **Teachable Moments** (the moral)
  - Prefer the version that feels natural — audiences detect "putting on a show."

## Key Concepts
- **Story arc / beats**: Named stages that create rise and fall.
- **Hidden value**: Unseen worth that Rags to Riches reveals.
- **Risk thermostat**: Adopter segment's appetite for newness.

## Mental Models
- Use Man in a Hole for recovery/lesson stories.
- Use No Easy Way when optimism alone would feel like a con.
- Use Pride & Fall to argue *with* the sceptic's inner narrative.
- Use Innovation Curve when the same idea needs different stories for different buyers.
- Use Five Ts when put on the spot.

## Anti-patterns
- **Only Rags to Riches** for sceptical audiences.
- **Blame-first failure stories** without learning.
- **One story for all adopter types**.
- **Performing** an unnatural Five Ts version.

## Key Takeaways
1. Arcs are reusable templates — map your project and your user's journey.
2. Realistic setbacks increase belief (No Easy Way).
3. Pre-empt Pride & Fall in the listener's head.
4. Match story to Innovation Curve segment.
5. Desert Island Structure card: **Man in a Hole**.
