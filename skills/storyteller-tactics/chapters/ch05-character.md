# Chapter 5: Character Tactics

## Core Idea
Character tactics answer *your role in the story*: earn trust, decode motivation, put the user as Hero and yourself as Guide.

## Frameworks Introduced

- **Trust Me, I'm an Expert** (Desert Island): Facts fail if actions contradict the Expert role. Tell a values-in-action story.
  - Prompts: What matters more than money? What would you never do? When did right action cost you? Failed-project obituary "at least we…"? Shared values with client shown in deeds?
  - Use ahead of Pitch Perfect.

- **What's My Motivation?** (Stanislavski): For each actor — Who / Where / When / Want / Why / How / Overcome. Then map Cooperation vs Conflict links. Pair with Good & Evil and No Easy Way.

- **Drive Stories** (credit: Pink, *Drive*): Team culture via Autonomy, Mastery, Purpose — positive and negative moments for each. Yields "how we work," "how we grow," "why we do it" stories.

- **Hero & Guide**: User = Hero; you = Expert Guide (not the star).
  - Guide types: Explorer (map/compass), Rebel (crowbar), Sage (book/crystal ball), Defender (armour), Muse (paint/canvas), Warrior (weapons/tactics).
  - Develop with Rags to Riches, Man in a Hole, or No Easy Way.

- **What's It About?**: Escape the curse of knowledge with three angles —
  - **Change** (responding vs initiating),
  - **New information** (something isn't right),
  - **Personal benefit** ("Ultimately I'm helping X achieve Y").
  - Then add Story Hooks. Pair with Audience Profile for beginners.

- **Cut to the Chase**: Emergency escape when the room goes dull.
  - Cut to **action** / **emotion** / **meaning**, or stop and ask a question (Story-ish Conversations).
  - Golden rule: "Thou shalt not be dull."

## Key Concepts
- **Curse of knowledge**: Experts can't find the entry point for newcomers.
- **Hero vs Guide**: Spotlight on the user; you enable.
- **Autonomy / Mastery / Purpose**: Intrinsic motivation story sources.

## Mental Models
- Use Trust Me when credibility is the bottleneck.
- Use Hero & Guide when you've been talking about yourself too much.
- Use What's It About for elevators, bios, email one-liners.
- Use Cut to the Chase as a live presentation bailout.

## Anti-patterns
- **Expert who doesn't live the values** (immunologist who won't vaccinate their kids).
- **Making yourself the Hero** of the customer's journey.
- **Backstory dumping** when attention is already gone.

## Key Takeaways
1. Trust is a story of character under cost, not a list of credentials.
2. Motivation mapping predicts how people will respond.
3. Put the user as Hero; pick your Guide gift deliberately.
4. One clear "what's it about" line unlocks every other tactic.
5. Desert Island Character card: **Trust Me, I'm an Expert**.
