# Exemplar — Connect Story Brief (fictional)

Composite example for agents. Not a real company.

```markdown
# Connect Story Brief
- Person / segment: “Devon,” lead barista-trainer at a 40-store coffee chain evaluating a new shift-scheduling tool. (Composite of 4 interviews.)
- Permission to share: Devon OK with anonymized internal research readout; no store number, no last name externally.
- Strong emotion + why it sticks: Quiet anger — not at corporate software, at being blamed when a opener calls out and the schedule “looked fine” on paper.
- Timeline (goal → what happened): Goal: open on time with certified people. Tuesday: two call-outs. Schedule showed coverage. Reality: one “covered” person wasn’t latte-art certified for the peak drink. Store opened late; Devon got the Slack from district.
- Key decisions + counterfactuals: Devon almost swapped in a willing closer (against policy). Didn’t. Counterfactual: if the schedule had shown certification gaps in red, the swap conversation happens Sunday, not at 5:55 a.m.
- Expertise a novice would miss: Coverage ≠ capability. Openers care about who can run bar *and* who has keys *and* who won’t melt during the first rush.
- Observed action vs what they say:
  - Says: “I just need better availability.”
  - Does: Hand-draws a side grid of certifications in a notebook every Sunday night.
- Universal trait in play: **Fear and bravery** — small act of protecting the team’s morning vs. protecting themselves from district blame.
- Life stage / archetype (Circle of Life): **Adult** in the Wide World — left “home” of being an individual contributor; not yet Parent/district. Conflict: Adult tools (spreadsheet) vs Parent rules (policy).
- Empathy story draft (their shoes):
  “Devon doesn’t open the app looking for ‘optimization.’ They open it scared of the one missing certification that turns a green schedule red in real life. Every Sunday they redraw the truth in a notebook the software can’t see. When Tuesday blows up, the tool still says everything was fine — so the blame lands on the human who already did the unpaid work.”
- What this means for the product / decision: Scheduling UI must surface capability constraints as first-class, not a buried filter. Notebook behavior is the product requirement.

```
